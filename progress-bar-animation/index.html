The One Book I Wish I Read Earlier

When you read regularly you come across a wide variety of books. A majority of the time the books that you read are impactful in the moment, but lose their potency as you move onto other books. However, occasionally you do come across a book that permeates through this barrier and stays with you.

The one book that had this impact on me was ‘12 Rules For Life’ by Jordan B. Peterson. Although he is a controversial figure in the political arena, the book holds a lot of value. As a follow-up to his first book ‘Maps of Meaning’, which is by far the most complicated piece of writing I have ever read. ‘12 Rules For Life’ sets out a framework for individuals to cultivate better self-awareness in order to live a better life.

Self-awareness is the key theme of the book. To look inwards and analyse the structure of your life to find what needs to be changed is a painful exercise. At times you are questioning every single habit or activity in order to assess whether it holds any value for long-term success. This is neither easy nor a quick process and requires time and diligent effort to see its rewards. What is evident is that this process, if done right, can lead to significant improvements in understanding who you actually are.

Rule 6 in the book, “Set your house in perfect order before you criticise the world” is a perfect assessment of this fact. When we are readily questioning the structures that govern us, however well intentioned, we are systematically not fixing the core issue. Individual sovereignty is the foundation of a democratic society, therefore it is up-to the individual to ensure that they are taking responsibility for their life.

When we are looking to Governments and Multi-National organisations to fix some of the worlds growing problems, it is important to also ask ourselves what we can do to fix the problem. This starts in the home and then permeates throughout society leading to more widespread change. Instead of blame for the system, it is aid in help of the system. The principles seem simplistic in their thought but difficult in their application.

As we look to find ways to improve the systems that govern us both individually and in society as a whole, individual accountability and sovereignty must be placed in the highest regard. A book such as ‘12 Rules For Life’ will help with this difficult exercise.
